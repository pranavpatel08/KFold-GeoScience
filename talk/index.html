Background

What is pore pressure?

The pressure of fluid trapped in the rock's pore spaces. While drilling, the mud must be heavy enough to hold that fluid back, but light enough not to crack the rock.
The stakes

Get the pressure wrong, and the well can blow out.

Deepwater Horizon, 2010 · Photo: U.S. Coast Guard (public domain)
  • A blowout is a loss of well control: the catastrophe the mud-weight window exists to prevent
  • Precise pore-pressure prediction is how you stay inside that window, before and during drilling
Blowouts are rare but catastrophic. Deepwater Horizon is the most visible reminder of why careful pre-drill pressure prediction matters.
Introduction

Pore pressure prediction is critical for drilling safety.

Overpressure deviates from hydrostatic, after Bowers (1995) [4]
  • Abnormal pressure drives about 20% of non-productive drilling time [1]
  • Nearly half the world's oil fields, 47.7%, are overpressured [2]
  • Physics models (Eaton, Bowers) assume tidy compaction and miss complex geology [3, 4]
Problem statement

ML reports R2 > 0.95. Most of it is leakage.

  • Data leakage: 78% of studies split randomly, so the same well lands in train and test [5]
  • Cross-well failure: tested honestly on blind wells, base models score negative R2 [6]
  • Open question: which models to include, and how to combine them?
Our fix: rank models out-of-fold, hold out whole wells, and never touch the test set during selection.

Methodology · Features & Models

13 features; five architecturally diverse models.

13 features
9 raw logs: TVD, DT, DT_NCT, GR, SPHI, HP, OB, RHOB, RES_DEEP

4 engineered: Eaton ratio, two pressure gradients, normalized depth
Five models
Neural nets: CNN, DFNN, RNN
Trees: Random Forest, XGBoost
Trees and neural nets fail differently. That diversity is the asset. Hyperparameters follow [6].
Methodology · K-Fold Stacking

Rank models out-of-fold, then combine.

K-Fold stacking [7]: 5-fold GroupKFold grouped by well, so no well appears in both a train and a validation fold.
Model selection uses out-of-fold metrics only. The blind wells are evaluated once, at the very end.
Results · Cross-Validation

Honest cross-well scores are compressed and low.

OOF R² · axis zoomed to 0.75–0.87
  • All five models land between 0.79 and 0.85, compressed and modest
  • These are realistic cross-well scores, with no well memorization
  • Models are ranked by out-of-fold R2, never by the test set
Results · Top-K Ablation

Performance improves monotonically through K = 5.

  • The biggest jump, +5.77 pp, comes from the first two models (XGBoost + DFNN)
  • The full ensemble reaches R2 = 0.8905, +7.03 pp over the best single model
  • Gains diminish after K = 2, but they never reverse
Results · Averaging vs. Stacking

Simple averaging beats Ridge stacking at every K.

  • Simple averaging wins at every size, K = 2, 3, 4 and 5
  • The gap widens with K, from 0.41 to 2.36 pp
  • At K = 5, averaging hits 0.8905 while Ridge falls to 0.8669
Discussion · Why averaging wins

Ridge discards diversity valuable under distribution shift.

Ridge coefficients at K = 5
  • Ridge fits out-of-fold predictions that are 0.92–0.97 correlated, so models look redundant
  • It piles weight on XGBoost and goes negative on RNN and RF, dropping them
  • Under distribution shift, those dropped models catch errors the others miss
  • Averaging keeps every model by construction, preserving the diversity
Results · Qualitative

Blind well PINDORI-2: the ensemble tracks the pressure profile.

  • The ensemble (red) tracks the actual pressure down the full depth column
  • Trees and neural nets miss in different places along the well
  • That complementary diversity is exactly what averaging preserves
Discussion · Limitations

What this does not yet show.

  • Single basin. All results are from one basin (Potwar, Pakistan); the exact numbers may not transfer, though the recipe should. Multi-basin validation is the priority.
  • One unconstrained combiner. We tested only unconstrained Ridge; a constrained combiner like non-negative least squares (NNLS) cannot discard models outright, and is the fair next baseline.
  • Only four blind wells, no error bars yet. Scores are point estimates on 4 wells; bootstrap confidence intervals are future work, so trust the trend over the exact gap.
  • Preprocessing is load-bearing. Physical-constraint filtering of the logs was needed to train the neural nets stably.
Conclusion

A leakage-free recipe for cross-well prediction.

  • The 5-model simple-average ensemble reaches R2 = 0.8905 on 4 blind wells, +7.03 pp over the best single model
  • Simple averaging beats Ridge, and the gap widens with K from 0.41 to 2.36 pp
  • Under distribution shift, prefer equal weighting over a learned combination. The recipe is domain-general; pore pressure is only our testbed.
  • Next: conformal uncertainty, SHAP explanations, and multi-basin validation
